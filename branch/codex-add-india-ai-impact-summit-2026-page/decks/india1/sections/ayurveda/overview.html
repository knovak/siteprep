Overview

Ayurveda

Ayurveda ("science of life") is India’s traditional medical system rooted in classical Sanskrit texts such as the Charaka Samhita and Sushruta Samhita. It centers on balancing the doshas (vata, pitta, kapha) through diet, lifestyle, herbal formulations, detox therapies (panchakarma), and bodywork.

Origins: Ancient India (codified c. 1st millennium BCE)
Core focus: Preventive care + chronic condition management
Government oversight: Ministry of AYUSH

Definition & history

  • Philosophy: Health is the balance of body, mind, and environment. Treatments are individualized to constitution (prakriti) and current imbalance (vikriti).
  • Key practices: Herbal formulas, therapeutic oils, cleansing protocols, yoga, meditation, and lifestyle prescriptions.
  • Historical arc: Royal court medicine in pre-colonial India, diminished during the colonial period, and revived in the 20th century through formal colleges and licensing.

Current status in India (numbers + scale)

  • Infrastructure scale: Recent AYUSH sector summaries cite roughly 3,500–3,700 Ayurveda hospitals and 27,000–30,000 Ayurveda dispensaries nationwide (government + private), reflecting one of the largest traditional medicine networks in the world.
  • Workforce: India maintains a large licensed practitioner base, commonly estimated in the 400,000+ range across public and private practice.
  • Wellness centers: Thousands of private Ayurveda clinics, resorts, and retreats operate alongside state-run facilities; Kerala alone hosts a dense concentration of certified Ayurveda hospitals and wellness resorts.

Where it is strongest (regional distribution)

  • Kerala: National hub for Ayurveda hospitals, panchakarma retreats, and medical tourism; coastal climate and long-standing institutional networks reinforce demand.
  • Karnataka & Tamil Nadu: Strong mix of teaching hospitals, private clinics, and urban wellness centers in Bengaluru, Mysuru, Chennai, and Coimbatore.
  • Maharashtra, Gujarat, Rajasthan: High density of outpatient clinics and traditional pharmacies, especially in larger cities and pilgrimage corridors.
  • North India metros: Delhi-NCR, Jaipur, and Chandigarh see rapid growth in integrative clinics and lifestyle-wellness programs.

Who uses Ayurveda

  • Local families: Preventive care, chronic pain, digestive issues, stress, and metabolic concerns.
  • Urban professionals: Short panchakarma resets, stress management, and sleep support.
  • Medical tourism: International visitors (especially to Kerala) for 7–21 day therapeutic programs that combine consultation, diet, and supervised treatments.

Prominent providers & brands

  • Arya Vaidya Sala (Kottakkal): Iconic Kerala-based hospital network and pharmacy.
  • Kerala Ayurveda / Somatheeram / Kairali: Well-known Kerala-based wellness and resort groups.
  • Ayurveda colleges + teaching hospitals: Common in Bengaluru, Mysuru, Chennai, Jaipur, and Delhi, providing lower-cost outpatient care.

Bengaluru notes

  • Urban availability: Many clinics operate in Indiranagar, Jayanagar, Koramangala, and Whitefield with appointment-based consultations.
  • Day-trip options: Resorts outside the city (Kanakapura Road, Ramanagara corridor) host multi-day detox and wellness packages.
